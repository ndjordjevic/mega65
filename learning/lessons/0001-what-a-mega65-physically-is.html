MEGA65 Hardware & FPGA · Lesson 01

What a MEGA65 physically is

The one idea that makes every later lesson click: the chips aren't chips.

Why this lesson: your mission is to understand the MEGA65 from the silicon up, ending in reading and writing the gateware. You can't read gateware until you know what the gate-array is pretending to be. Today's win: draw the MEGA65 block diagram from memory.

A 1991 Commodore 65 had a circuit board strewn with a dozen custom chips — a CPU, a video chip, sound chips — each one permanently etched in silicon.1 The MEGA65 recreates that whole machine, but with almost none of those chips present. Instead, one modern programmable chip becomes all of them.

The core idea: gateware, not silicon

At the center of the board sits a Xilinx Artix-7 FPGA — a field-programmable gate array. An FPGA is a sea of logic gates whose wiring is decided by a configuration file, not by the factory. Load a different file and the same physical chip behaves like completely different hardware.1

So the MEGA65's CPU, video chip, and sound chips are not parts you could desolder. They are descriptions — written in a hardware description language (VHDL) — that the Artix-7 turns into live digital logic when it powers on. That description is the core (the gateware). This is the whole reason your end-goal — reading and writing that VHDL — is even possible.

FPGAs on the board: one

On the current production board — R6, shipping since 2024 — there is exactly one FPGA: the Artix-7.3 Everything else on the board is ordinary fixed-function electronics.

Xilinx Artix-7 — the machine

Runs the core that implements the entire C65-class chipset. "The MEGA65 core" = the configuration file loaded into this chip at power-on. It's the one device on the board that runs gateware — everything else is fixed-function silicon.3

Takeaway

Every line of gateware in a current MEGA65 lives in one repo: mega65-core (VHDL, built with Xilinx Vivado). One chip, one codebase — that's the whole target of your journey.

Check your own unit: freeze the machine (RESTORE, long press) and press HELP — the MEGA Info tool reports your board revision, core and ROM versions.4

What the Artix-7 turns into

Inside that one chip, the core implements the custom devices that would have been separate silicon:2

  • 45GS02 — the CPU (an extended 6502-family processor), ~40× the speed of a C64.
  • VIC-IV — the video chip; drives VGA and HDMI output.
  • 4× SID — the sound chips (the legendary Commodore sound, four-fold).
  • 45E100 — a fast Ethernet controller.

We'll devote a later lesson to each. Today they're just labels inside the box.

Memory & storage: beyond the C65's own RAM, the MEGA65 adds "Attic RAM" — 8 MiB of extra memory available to software (physically backed by a larger 64 MiB chip on the R6 board).4 For storage, there are two SD-card slots.4

Check yourself (retrieval practice)

Answer out loud before opening each — effortful recall is what makes it stick.

How many FPGAs are on the R6 board, and which one?

One — the Xilinx Artix-7 (part XC7A200T), which is the computer. Everything else on the board is fixed-function silicon.

Why can't you point to "the CPU chip" on the board?

Because the CPU (45GS02) isn't a physical chip — it's VHDL gateware running inside the Artix-7 FPGA. The same is true of the VIC-IV, the SIDs, and the 45E100.

Name the four custom devices the Artix-7 implements.

45GS02 (CPU), VIC-IV (video), 4× SID (sound), 45E100 (ethernet).

Where does all the gateware of a current MEGA65 live?

In one repo: mega65-core — the VHDL that gets built (with Xilinx Vivado) into the configuration loaded onto the Artix-7. That single codebase is the end-goal of this learning track.

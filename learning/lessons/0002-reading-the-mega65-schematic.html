MEGA65 Hardware & FPGA · Lesson 02

Reading the MEGA65 schematic

Lesson 01 said "one FPGA becomes the chipset." The schematic shows you exactly how it touches the real world.

Why this lesson: the schematic is the board's ground truth — every chip, wire, and connector, drawn by the people who built it. By the end you'll be able to (1) open the official R6 schematic and navigate it like a map, and (2) point to where each device from Lesson 01 lives and what it connects to. This is the bridge from "what a MEGA65 is" to "how it actually works."

A schematic is not a picture of the board — it's a diagram of connections. It throws away physical layout and keeps only what's electrically true: which pin of which chip joins which net. Learn to read one and the machine stops being a mystery box.

Where the schematic lives

The official MEGA65 schematic is a PDF that ships inside the User's Guide source repo:1

  • raw/github/MEGA65-mega65-user-guide/assets/TE0765-06.PDF — the R6 schematic, 21 sheets.
  • Older revisions sit beside it: TE0765-03.PDF (R3), TE0765-02.PDF (R2).
  • It's pulled into the books by appendix-schematics.tex (the Developer's Guide "Schematics" chapter).

"TE0765" is the board's part number. The MEGA65 mainboard is manufactured by Trenz Electronic; TE0765 is their model, and the -06 suffix is the R6 revision — the same board Lesson 01 locked onto. The title block on every sheet also reads "Rev. 06," dated 2023-12-14, and the design is released as open hardware under the CERN-OHL-P v2.0 licence.1

Part A — How to read any schematic

Before touring the MEGA65 specifically, five reading skills. They apply to every schematic you'll ever open.

1 · It's hierarchical — start at the top sheet

A board this size isn't one giant drawing. It's a top sheet that lists sub-sheets, each covering one function. Think of it as a table of contents. The MEGA65's top sheet names 20 sub-sheets — HDMI, VGA, Ethernet, SOUND, KEYBOARD, POWER, EXP_Slot, and so on — plus housekeeping like mounting holes and shielding.1

2 · Every sheet has a title block (bottom-right)

The boxed block in the corner of each sheet tells you where you are: the project (MEGA65), the board number (TE0765), the Rev. (06), the date, the page N of 23, and the filename of that sub-sheet (e.g. HDMI.SchDoc). When someone says "see the sound sheet," this is how you find it — and how you confirm you're reading the right revision.

3 · The grid locates parts — "U10 is at B2"

The numbers across the top/bottom (1–4 or 1–8) and letters down the sides (A–D) form a coordinate grid, like a paper map. A part or note is referenced by its square — "the level shifter at C3." On a dense sheet this is the only sane way to point at something.

4 · Wires you can't see: net labels & power rails

Not every connection is a drawn line. A net label — a name like SDRAM_DQ0, C64_D7, or AUDIO_BCLK — means "this wire is electrically the same as every other wire with this name," even across different sheets. That's how the FPGA on one sheet reaches the SDRAM on another without a literal wire between them. Special cases you'll see constantly:

  • Power rails: 3.3V, 5V, 1.8V, 1V — the voltages that feed everything.
  • Ground: GND — the common return; the most-repeated label on the board.
  • Off-sheet connectors: the little flag symbols (B13, B16, B34…) carry whole groups of signals to the matching flag on another sheet.

5 · Reference designators name the parts

Every component has a refdes — a letter for its type plus a number:

U = IC/chip · R = resistor · C = capacitor · L = inductor/ferrite · D = diode/LED · T = transistor · J = connector/jack · S = switch · Y/oscillator = clock.

You'll notice swarms of small capacitors next to every chip — those are decoupling capacitors, steadying the power supply right at each chip's pins. Once you learn to mentally skip them, the real signal connections jump out.

One last representational trick: a single large part is often drawn as several symbols across different sheets. A big chip or a many-pin connector gets sliced into "units" sharing one number with letter suffixes — U1A, U1B, U1C… — all the same physical part. So when you see a lettered refdes, look for its siblings elsewhere. On the MEGA65 this trick matters more than on almost any board — which is exactly where Part B begins.

Part B — The board by function (the logical view)

This is the board grouped by what it does — the way an engineer thinks about it. We start at the center (the FPGA), then walk outward to memory, boot, and every port. Each block maps to something you met in Lesson 01, but now you can see the actual silicon. (Part C then walks the same board the other way — page by page, as the schematic is literally drawn.)

The center · the FPGA, drawn as its banks

This is the insight that ties back to Lesson 01. The FPGA — Xilinx XC7A200T-2FBG484C — appears on many sheets as U1A, U1B, U1C … up to U1J. These are not ten chips. They are one physical chip, U1, split across sheets because a 484-ball BGA package has far too many pins to draw in a single symbol. Altium (the CAD tool) lets you slice one part into multiple "units," and the MEGA65 splits U1 by I/O bank.3

So the FPGA's pins are grouped by bank, and each bank gets its own sheet — this is the spine the rest of the tour hangs off:

  • U1A = Bank 13 · U1B = Bank 14 · U1C = Bank 15 · U1D = Bank 16 — general I/O.
  • U1E = Bank 34 · U1F = Bank 35 — high-speed I/O (SDRAM, HDMI, Ethernet).
  • U1G = Bank 0 — configuration/boot pins.
  • U1H = Bank 216 — gigabit transceivers (mostly unused on MEGA65).
  • U1I / U1J — the FPGA's own power and ground balls.

Seeing "the chip is the banks" makes the rest of the schematic legible: each I/O sheet is really "what does this slice of the FPGA connect to?" — which is precisely the question each block below answers.

Block 1 · Power — from barrel jack to FPGA rails

Think of this as a step-down chain: raw wall power gets refined into progressively smaller, cleaner voltages the closer it gets to the FPGA — because the chip can't run on messy high voltage directly.3

  1. Barrel jack (J11) — where the external power adapter plugs in.
  2. Protection — a fuse, a surge-absorbing diode, and an over/under-voltage guard (the LTC4365, U34) that only lets power through when it's in a safe range.
  3. Main 5 V rail — a buck regulator (the MP8869, U12) steps the input down to a steady 5 V — the board's main supply, which most peripherals run on directly.
  4. FPGA core rails — three more regulators turn that 5 V into the voltages the Artix-7 needs internally: 1.0 V for the logic core, 1.8 V for its auxiliary circuitry, and 3.3 V for all its I/O pins.

Block 2 · The clock — one 100 MHz crystal

This is the board's heartbeat — everything inside the FPGA needs to tick in sync, and it all starts from a single source:

  1. One oscillator — the SiT8008 (U17) does nothing but produce a steady, precise pulse: 100 million ticks per second (100 MHz).
  2. Into a clock-capable FPGA pin — a pin specifically built to receive a fast timing signal and distribute it internally.
  3. Multiplied/divided inside the FPGA — dedicated clock-management blocks fan that one 100 MHz reference out into every clock the design needs — the CPU clock, the pixel clock, the audio clock, and more.3

One crystal in, many clocks synthesised on-chip — no need for a dozen separate oscillators on the board.

Block 3 · Memory — SDRAM (the Attic RAM) + HyperRAM

Lesson 01's "Attic RAM" turns into two real, physical chips here — each wired straight to the FPGA at high speed, on a different I/O bank:

  • SDRAM — an ISSI IS42S16320 (U44), 64 MiB, on Bank 34. The R6 Attic-RAM backing store (of which the core currently maps 8 MiB; see Lesson 01).3
  • HyperRAM — an ISSI IS66WVH8M8 (U29), 8 MiB, on Bank 16. The older, smaller memory that the SDRAM was added (at revision R4) to surpass.3

Block 4 · Boot — how the core gets into the FPGA

An FPGA is blank silicon — it forgets everything when powered off, so every single power-up it has to be told from scratch which circuit to become:3

  1. Where the design lives — a Cypress S25FL512S QSPI flash (U5A), a separate storage chip next to the FPGA holding the core bitstream(s) — this is where your "core slots" physically live.
  2. Boot mode — master SPI — at power-up the FPGA reads its own configuration directly from that flash over SPI (Serial Peripheral Interface, a simple protocol for one chip to pull data from another).
  3. JTAG — the developer side-door — a header that lets a PC push a new bitstream straight into the FPGA, bypassing the flash — how developers flash a new core onto the board in the first place.

The pattern: the FPGA is blank at every power-on → reads its "personality" from a dedicated flash chip → becomes whatever core was stored there.

Block 5 · Video out — VIC-IV → VGA & HDMI

This introduces a pattern you'll see repeated for audio and Ethernet too: the FPGA generates the content, while a dedicated chip handles the physical signal going out the connector.

  • The VIC-IV (gateware, inside the FPGA) — the video-generation logic; it produces digital R/G/B pixel data.
  • VGA — the ADV7125 (U3) — a video DAC (Digital-to-Analog Converter). VGA monitors expect analog voltage levels, not digital bits, so this chip converts the FPGA's digital R/G/B into the analog signal the VGA connector needs.3
  • HDMI — the PTN3363 (U10) — HDMI is digital too, but uses a high-speed encoding called TMDS; this chip cleans up and re-drives the FPGA's TMDS signal so it's strong enough for the HDMI cable.3

The MEGA65 can drive both ports from the same VIC-IV source at once — VGA for older analog displays, HDMI for modern digital ones. Same data, two different physical translations.

Block 6 · Audio — the SID sound → a real DAC

Same pattern again: the FPGA generates the sound data, and a dedicated chip turns it into an actual analog signal you can hear.

  • Four SIDs (gateware, inside the FPGA) — the legendary C64 sound chip, recreated four times over in FPGA logic — enough for stereo and richer multi-voice sound than one original SID could do.
  • Mixed and sent over I2S — the FPGA combines all four into one digital audio stream and sends it out over I2S (Inter-IC Sound — SPI's audio-specialized cousin).
  • The AK4432 (U37) — a stereo audio DAC that converts that digital stream into analog voltage for the 3.5 mm jack and RCA outputs.3

This high-quality DAC is itself a later-revision upgrade over earlier, simpler audio hardware — see the evolution table below.

Block 7 · Ethernet — 45E100 → PHY → RJ45

The same FPGA-does-protocol / chip-does-wire pattern, this time for networking.

  • The 45E100 (gateware, inside the FPGA) — the Ethernet controller logic: framing packets and managing the protocol, but still in a simplified internal digital format.
  • The KSZ8081 (U4) — a dedicated Ethernet PHY (physical-layer) chip that converts the FPGA's simplified signals into the real electrical signaling an Ethernet cable requires.3
  • RJ45 jack (J10A) — with built-in magnetics (tiny transformers that electrically isolate the board from the cable).

Same shape as video and audio: the FPGA owns the flexible protocol logic; a fixed-function chip next door meets the connector's exact electrical spec.

Block 8 · Storage, keyboard, and the legacy ports

The rest of Lesson 01's port list — this block is a grab-bag, since each of these is a genuinely separate physical port:

  • SD cards — two sockets, micro (SD1) and full-size (SD2). Two slots let you keep system/core files on one card and your own disk images on the other.
  • Keyboard (J5) — the link out to the MK-II keyboard's own electronics, which read the key matrix through I2C expanders inside the keyboard housing (Lesson 01).
  • Cartridge / expansion port — exposes the full C64-compatible bus so old cartridges and expansion hardware can plug in. It's dense with level shifters because the original C64 bus runs at 5 V while the FPGA's I/O is 3.3 V.
  • Joysticks — two DE-9 ports (the same connector as a real C64 joystick) with level shifters and a switchable 5 V supply — needed because analog paddles and mice draw power through this same port.
  • Floppy & IEC — a 34-pin internal floppy header for a real floppy mechanism, plus the IEC serial port for classic 1541-style external drives.

The throughline: these are the "talk to old/external C64 hardware" ports — voltage-translating, bus-exposing connectors — as opposed to Blocks 5–7, which generate new digital media for modern devices.

Block 9 · The quiet helpers — EEPROMs, clock, revision ID

The housekeeping chips — small, slow support chips the board can't function properly without, even though none of them do anything flashy. All four share one I2C bus (a simple 2-wire bus — clock + data — that many chips can share, each picked out by its own address):3

  • Config EEPROM — stores settings that need to survive power-off, separate from the big QSPI flash that holds the actual FPGA core.
  • MAC-address EEPROM — holds the board's factory-assigned Ethernet MAC address, the unique ID every network device needs.
  • Real-time clock — kept ticking by its own CR2032 coin cell even when the board is unplugged — the same idea as the clock battery on a PC motherboard.
  • I/O expander — gives the FPGA extra digital inputs cheaply over I2C instead of spending dedicated pins on them. Here it reads the DIP switches and reports the board revision in hardware, so the running core can ask "what board am I actually on?" instead of guessing.

That last point matters beyond this board: it's literally how the MEGA65 core detects its own hardware revision at runtime — the same reason this lesson insists on qualifying facts like "64 MiB SDRAM" by revision (see the evolution table below).

Part C — The schematic, page by page (the literal document)

Part B grouped the board by function. Part C is the other lens: every sheet of the actual PDF, in order (pages 3→23), so after this you can open any page and know what every major part is. For each sheet: its purpose, the drawing, and a high-level component list — chips and connectors, not every wire.

Click any drawing to open it full-size. Refdes reminder: U chip · J connector · R/C/L passives · D diode/LED · T transistor · S switch.

Recurring helper parts (so the lists below can stay short — each does one simple job): buffer (NC7SZ126, 74AHCT1G125) — drives or isolates a single signal; level shifter (SN74LVCH16T245) — bridges the FPGA's 3.3 V to the 5 V legacy bus; load switch (AP2196, MP5010, MP5077) — turns a power rail on or off under control; MOSFET (2N7002) — a tiny transistor used as an on/off switch; TVS / clamp diode (SMBJ, WE-TVS, BAV199) — shunts voltage spikes to protect a port; decoupling cap — the cap swarms next to every chip, steadying its supply.

How the MEGA65 board evolved (R1 → R6)

The board you're reading is one snapshot in a decade of revisions. The MEGA65 began as an FPGA experiment on hobbyist dev boards and matured into the Trenz-built desktop machine — and crucially, hardware facts depend on which revision you mean. The early "three FPGAs" and "8 MiB only" descriptions were true once and aren't now. Here's the arc, from the User's Guide's "Model Specific Features" appendix:2

RevYear / useWhat changed
R1earliest prototypeFirst desktop prototype hardware (id $01).
R2prototypeDesktop board with battery-backed RTC + NVRAM, an ADV7511 digital-video chip, and a mono PC speaker amp.
R3 / R3ADevKit 2020; production 2022–23Dropped the ADV7511; stereo speaker amp. R3A is the same PCB with a bigger QSPI flash — 8 core slots instead of 4.
R4major reworkRemoved the Intel MAX10 helper FPGA entirely — the second on-board programmable chip — simplifying the design and removing a failure mode. Added a 64 MiB SDRAM alongside the 8 MiB HyperRAM, a high-quality audio DAC, the new RV-3032 RTC, joystick drive-low capability, and the J21 debug header.
R5supply-chain reworkMinor rework of R4 (power-regulator parts). Made several expansion-port signals bidirectional, added 4 more DIP switches (8 total) behind an I2C expander, and exposed the board revision in hardware via that expander.
R6production 2024 — this boardElectrically identical to R5. The current shipping desktop MEGA65, and the schematic this lesson reads.

The "three FPGAs" story, accurately: R2/R3 really did have three programmable chips — the Artix-7, the Intel MAX10 helper (removed at R4, above), and a Lattice chip in the MK-I keyboard. The keyboard's chip went away in a separate redesign — the MK-II keyboard reads its matrix through plain I2C IO-expanders instead (Lesson 01). So today's R6 desktop + MK-II keyboard has exactly one FPGA, the Artix-7 — verified against this very schematic.2

There's also a handheld branch — the MEGAphone (id $21) — with a touch screen, accelerometer, and I2C-controlled power, plus FPGA dev-board targets (Nexys4DDR, QMTECH) for builders without a real board. A running MEGA65 reports its own revision: the core reads it from the I2C expander, and you can check yours via $D629 after enabling the MEGA65 I/O personality.2

Takeaway for accuracy: whenever you state a MEGA65 hardware fact, anchor it to a revision. "The MEGA65 has a 64 MiB SDRAM" is an R4-and-later fact; "three FPGAs" was an R2/R3 fact. This lesson is R6 throughout.

Check yourself (retrieval practice)

Answer out loud before opening each.

On the schematic, U1A through U1J appear on many sheets. How many chips is that?

One. They're all units of U1, the single Xilinx XC7A200T FPGA, split by I/O bank because the 484-ball package has too many pins to draw as one symbol.

You see the label SDRAM_DQ0 on two different sheets with no wire between them. Are they connected?

Yes — identical net labels are the same electrical wire, even across sheets. That's how the FPGA on one sheet reaches the SDRAM on another.

The VIC-IV is gateware inside the FPGA. So what are the ADV7125 and PTN3363 chips for?

Physical output. The FPGA generates the video; the ADV7125 is the analog VGA DAC and the PTN3363 drives the HDMI TMDS lines. The FPGA does protocol; dedicated chips do the wire. (Same pattern as the KSZ8081 Ethernet PHY for the 45E100.)

Where does the core come from at power-on, and which sheet shows it?

From the S25FL512S QSPI flash (U5A) on the FPGA-CFG sheet, in MASTER SPI boot mode. JTAG is the alternate path for loading a bitstream from a PC.

Why is "the MEGA65 has 64 MiB of RAM" an incomplete statement?

Because it's revision-specific. The 64 MiB SDRAM arrived at R4; R2/R3 had only the 8 MiB HyperRAM. Always anchor a hardware fact to a revision.
